Coffee, brewed
four ways.

Coffee's flavor is shaped as much by how you brew it as by the beans themselves. Grind size, water temperature, and contact time decide whether your cup tastes bright, balanced, or bitter — a short walk through the methods that matter.

Method — 02 Filtered · Manual
Drip · Pour-Over

A clean, bright cup — built drop by drop.

Pour-over brewing — V60, Chemex, Kalita — passes hot water through ground coffee in a paper filter. The result is a cup that is clean, bright, and remarkably nuanced. The brewer controls everything: flow rate, saturation, bloom, and timing.

Method — 03 Immersion · Unfiltered
French Press

Full body, forgiving — steep, plunge, pour.

The French press steeps coarsely ground coffee directly in hot water for about four minutes, then separates the grounds with a metal mesh plunger. Without a paper filter, more oils and fine particles remain in the cup — producing a heavier, rounder mouthfeel.

Method — 04 Pressure · Concentrated
Espresso

Pressure, time, crema — concentrated by force.

Espresso forces hot water through finely ground, tightly packed coffee under ~9 bars of pressure, in roughly 25–30 seconds. The result is a small, intense shot crowned with crema — the foundation for lattes, cappuccinos, and Americanos.

Method — 05 Cold extraction · Slow
Cold Brew

Slow, cold, naturally sweet — patience as a recipe.

Cold brew steeps coarse grounds in cold or room-temperature water for 12–24 hours, then filters out the grounds. The slow, cold extraction yields a smooth, low-acid concentrate with a natural sweetness — served over ice, easy to make in batches.
